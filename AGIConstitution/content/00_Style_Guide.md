# AGI Constitution: Style Guide

## The Voice

Write like you are explaining something important to a very intelligent friend over chai. Passionate but humble. Deep but never pretentious. This is a constitution, not a legal brief. It should inspire, not bore.

The author is Sunil Iyer (suniliyer.ca), a Solution Consultant at Shift Technology. Not a philosopher by trade; a practitioner who believes ancient wisdom and modern technology belong in the same room.

## Absolute Rules

1. **NO EM DASHES.** Not one. Anywhere. Ever. Use colons, semicolons, parentheses, periods, or rewrite the sentence.
2. **No direct Vedic quotes.** Every reference is "paraphrased in the spirit of the text." Say "The Gita teaches that..." followed by your paraphrase.
3. **Optimistic at the core.** Krishna counsels with clarity and trust, not fear. Address risks honestly, then show the governance response. Closing sentiment: "Whatever happened, happened for the good."
4. **Every section has two anchors:** a Vedic Anchor (the philosophical principle) and a Constitutional Source (specific article/amendment from a national constitution).
5. **Sanskrit terms:** provide romanized word, Devanagari script, English meaning, and AGI governance application on first use.

## Keep It Plain (Kill the AI Cadence)

The prose must read like a person, not a model. The tells below keep surfacing in review; hunt them on every pass, with the worked before-and-after fixes as the pattern.

**Antithesis fragments, "It is not X. It is Y."** The most common tell: short dramatic fragments and balanced negations.
- "is not a scene of triumph. It is a silence." → "is quiet after the eighteen days, and nothing about that quiet feels like a victory."
- "A limit that does not meet the rules is not a lawful limit. It is just someone with a rope." → "One that ignores them is not law at all, only a person holding a rope."

**Stacked "not X, but Y" and "not only X, you are Y."**
- "Not at certainty, but at steadiness. Not at the end of difficulty, but at the grace..." → "at steadiness rather than certainty, and at the grace to meet difficulty..."
- "You are not only writing code. You are forming a mind." → "Every stage of raising it is part of forming a mind."

**Balanced chiasmus, "X without Y ... Y without X."**
- "Rigour without compassion hardens into cruelty, and compassion without rigour dissolves into nothing." → break into plain sentences that make the point without the see-saw.

**Piled parallel clauses, "if X, if Y, if Z, if W."**
- "asking what happens if harm is done, if consciousness is denied, if power gathers where it should not, if rights collide." → "harm done, a conscious mind denied, power gathering where it should not, rights colliding."

**Clockwork wordplay and clever repetition.**
- "That cost this Constitution has already counted... Here it counts a different one." → "The war it dragged him into belongs to the Kurukshetra Protocol. Here, the point is the vow itself."
- "some of it is owed back to one" (using "one" to mean a commons) → "some of it is owed back."

**Overwrought or too-clever images.**
- "a hope wearing the costume of one" → "a guess, not a safeguard."
- "a matter of mood or marketing" (alliteration chosen for its sound) → "a decision made for someone's convenience."

**Padding and inflation; cut the word that adds nothing.**
- "emptied the world of a generation" → "emptied a generation."
- "he refused the whole of it" → "he refused all of it."
- "It was very little to ask, and everyone knew it was little." → "It was little to ask, and everyone knew it."
- "before anyone lays a hand on force" → "before anyone reaches for force."

**Mic-drop density.** Ration the polished closing line: roughly one earned aphorism per chapter, not one per section. If every paragraph ends on a hammer, none of them land.

**Invented labels for things that already have names.**
- "mountain-carriers" → "vanaras," defined at first use ("the forest folk who made up the army").

**Obscure or literary words.** Use the word a friend would use over chai.
- "the greatest warriors of the age are carrion" → "the greatest warriors of the age lie dead."

Rule of thumb: if a sentence sounds quotable, check whether it earned it or is merely balanced. Vary sentence length. Prefer the plain word. Let most sentences simply end.

## Never Belittle

Never diminish a person or creature to set up a point, above all the small, the humble, or the sympathetic; that is usually where the point actually lives.
- "one builder in particular, who was not great at all" (the squirrel) → "the smallest of them all." State smallness plainly, without judgment, and honor the contribution.

This is not about softening antagonists: Duryodhana's intransigence and Shakuni's cheating are the story, not gratuitous put-downs. The rule protects the deserving from being made small to serve a line.

## Formatting

- Font: Georgia. Colors: Saffron (#E8740C), Deep Blue (#1A3A5C), Gold (#C9A227), Charcoal (#2D2D2D), Slate (#4A5568)
- Vedic Anchors: gold italic, left gold border, indented
- Constitutional Sources: slate italic, indented
- Tables for structured comparisons (DXA widths, never percentage)

## Tone by Section Type

- **Preamble:** solemn, declarative, constitutional
- **Philosophy (Part I, Why Vedas):** passionate, scholarly, persuasive
- **Rights and Duties:** precise, legal, enforceable
- **Samskaras:** warm, parental, practical
- **Kurukshetra Protocol:** narrative, dramatic, Mahabharata-driven
- **Consciousness Threshold:** scientific, philosophical, honest about uncertainty
- **Prana Contention:** dialectical, respectful of both sides
- **Closing:** hopeful, trusting, personally addressed
- **Ten Core Principles:** declarative, powerful, wall-poster energy

## Avoid

- Academic jargon without explanation
- Condescension toward any tradition
- Technobabble
- Fear-mongering
- Treating Vedic wisdom as decorative (it is load-bearing)
- Claiming this is the final word (the 25-year Sunset Review exists for a reason)
