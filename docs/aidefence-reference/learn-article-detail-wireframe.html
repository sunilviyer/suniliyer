The Building Blocks ‚Äì What AI Actually Is

fundamentals definitions OECD ISO

AI is machine-based systems that infer from inputs to generate outputs influencing physical or virtual environments. This definition from the OECD (November 2023) provides the foundation for understanding AI governance, regulation, and responsible development.

OECD AI Definition (Nov 2023)

The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) updated its AI definition in November 2023 to reflect the rapid evolution of AI systems. This definition is now the international standard used by governments, regulatory bodies, and industry organizations worldwide.

"An AI system is a machine-based system that, for explicit or implicit objectives, infers, from the input it receives, how to generate outputs such as predictions, content, recommendations, or decisions that can influence physical or virtual environments."

This definition is deliberately broad to encompass both current and future AI systems. It highlights four key characteristics:

  • Machine-based: AI runs on computational infrastructure, distinguishing it from human intelligence
  • Inference capability: AI systems learn patterns and make inferences rather than following explicit programming
  • Output generation: AI produces various outputs including predictions, content, recommendations, and decisions
  • Environmental influence: AI outputs can affect both physical systems (robots, autonomous vehicles) and virtual environments (recommendations, content moderation)

ISO/IEC 22989:2022 Terminology

The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) and the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) published ISO/IEC 22989:2022, establishing standardized terminology for artificial intelligence concepts and techniques.

This standard provides the technical vocabulary needed for AI governance, including definitions for:

  • Machine Learning (ML): Systems that improve performance through experience
  • Neural Networks: Computing systems inspired by biological neural networks
  • Deep Learning: ML using neural networks with multiple layers
  • Natural Language Processing (NLP): AI systems that understand and generate human language
  • Computer Vision: Systems that derive information from visual inputs

ISO/IEC 22989:2022 ensures that AI practitioners, regulators, and policymakers use consistent terminology when discussing AI systems, reducing ambiguity in governance frameworks.

What Makes AI Different from Traditional Software

Understanding what distinguishes AI from traditional software is crucial for effective governance. Traditional software follows explicit, deterministic rules written by programmers. AI systems, in contrast, learn patterns from data and make probabilistic inferences.

Key differences include:

  • Development approach: Traditional software uses explicit programming; AI uses training on data
  • Behavior predictability: Traditional software is deterministic; AI is probabilistic and can exhibit emergent behaviors
  • Explainability: Traditional software logic is transparent; AI decision-making can be opaque (the "black box" problem)
  • Adaptability: Traditional software requires manual updates; AI can continue learning and adapting
  • Error patterns: Traditional software has bugs; AI has bias, hallucinations, and distributional shift issues

These differences mean that AI governance cannot simply adapt existing software governance frameworks. New approaches are needed to address AI-specific risks.

Why Definitions Matter for Governance

Precise definitions are the foundation of effective AI governance. When governments, companies, and international bodies discuss AI regulation, they must share a common understanding of what qualifies as an "AI system."

Clear definitions enable:

  • Regulatory scope: Determining which systems fall under AI-specific regulations (e.g., EU AI Act)
  • Risk assessment: Identifying which AI systems pose high risks requiring additional oversight
  • Compliance frameworks: Creating testable requirements for AI safety and transparency
  • International coordination: Enabling cross-border cooperation on AI governance
  • Public accountability: Helping citizens understand when AI is being used to make decisions affecting their lives

The OECD and ISO definitions provide this shared foundation, enabling coherent global approaches to AI governance while allowing individual jurisdictions to tailor specific requirements to their contexts.
